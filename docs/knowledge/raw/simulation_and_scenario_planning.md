# Simulation and Scenario Planning — Supply Chain Knowledge

## Purpose of Simulation in Supply Chains

Real supply chains operate in uncertain environments.

Demand changes.  
Suppliers fail.  
Transportation delays happen.  
Promotions create unexpected spikes.

Because of this uncertainty, companies do not rely only on static planning.  
They run **scenario simulations** to understand how their planning policies behave when conditions change.

Simulation answers the key operational question:

"What happens to our system if the world changes?"

Instead of waiting for disruptions to occur in reality, simulation allows planners to **stress test policies in advance**.

This is why simulation is widely used in retail, manufacturing, and logistics planning systems.

---

# Scenario Planning

Scenario planning is the practice of testing multiple possible future situations.

Each scenario modifies some part of the system and observes the impact.

Examples:

Baseline scenario  
Normal operating conditions.

Demand spike scenario  
Demand increases unexpectedly.

Promotion scenario  
Marketing campaign increases product demand.

Supplier delay scenario  
Lead time from suppliers increases.

Inventory shock scenario  
Warehouse stock becomes unavailable due to damage or logistics issues.

Scenario planning allows organizations to evaluate whether their supply chain policies remain effective under uncertainty.

---

# Why Scenario Simulation Matters

Supply chain policies such as replenishment rules are usually designed using assumptions.

Example assumptions:

Lead time = 5 days  
Demand variability = stable  
Supplier reliability = high

If those assumptions change, the policy may no longer work.

Simulation helps answer:

• Does the replenishment policy still protect service levels?  
• Which scenarios create the highest stockout risk?  
• Which disruptions require policy adjustments?  

This is known as **policy stress testing**.

---

# Demand Shock Scenarios

Demand shocks occur when customer demand suddenly changes.

Common causes:

• promotions  
• viral trends  
• seasonal spikes  
• panic buying  

Example

Normal daily demand = 100 units  
Demand spike scenario = 150 units

Effects:

• inventory depletes faster  
• reorder points may become insufficient  
• stockouts become more likely

Mental Hook

Demand shocks test whether forecasting and replenishment policies can **handle sudden demand acceleration**.

---

# Supply Disruption Scenarios

Supply disruptions affect the ability to restock inventory.

Common causes:

• supplier production delays  
• shipping congestion  
• transportation failures  
• geopolitical events  

Example

Normal lead time = 5 days  
Disruption scenario = 12 days

Effects:

• replenishment orders arrive late  
• safety stock may become insufficient  
• service levels decline

Mental Hook

Supply disruptions test whether the system is **resilient to delays in restocking inventory**.

---

# Inventory Shock Scenarios

Inventory shocks represent unexpected loss or misalignment of inventory.

Examples:

• warehouse damage  
• inventory shrinkage  
• logistics misrouting  
• system counting errors  

Example

Inventory position = 100 units  
Shock scenario = inventory drops to 60 units

Effects:

• stockout risk increases  
• replenishment decisions must react quickly

Mental Hook

Inventory shocks test how quickly the system can **recover from sudden inventory loss**.

---

# Policy Sensitivity Analysis

Scenario simulation is often used to analyze how sensitive supply chain policies are to changes.

Example questions:

• What happens if demand increases by 20%?  
• What happens if lead time doubles?  
• What happens if safety stock is reduced?

This process is known as **sensitivity analysis**.

It helps planners understand which parameters most strongly influence system performance.

Mental Hook

Sensitivity analysis answers:

"Which variables can break our supply chain policy?"

---

# Scenario Comparison

After executing multiple scenarios, planners compare the outcomes.

Common evaluation metrics:

• stockout rate  
• service level  
• fill rate  
• inventory levels  
• replenishment volume

Example comparison question:

Which scenario produces the highest stockout risk?

Which scenario generates the largest replenishment orders?

Scenario comparison transforms the supply chain model into a **decision evaluation system**.

---

# Role of Simulation in Modern Supply Chain Systems

Modern planning systems use simulation for several purposes:

• policy testing  
• disruption preparedness  
• supply chain resilience analysis  
• decision support

Simulation helps organizations move from **reactive operations** to **proactive planning**.

Mental Hook

Simulation turns the supply chain system into a **controlled experiment environment**.

Instead of learning only from real disruptions, the system can learn from simulated ones.

---

# Key Mental Model

A supply chain planning system answers three core questions:

1. What demand should we expect?  
2. What is our current inventory risk?  
3. What decisions should we take?

Simulation extends this with a fourth question:

"What happens if conditions change?"

This makes simulation one of the most powerful tools for evaluating supply chain policies.
